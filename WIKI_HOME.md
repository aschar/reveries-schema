# Rêveries Dream Enrichment Schema

## A note before the schema

There is a risk in building a schema for dreams: the risk is that the act of structuring dream content, of assigning fields and tiers and confidence scores, begins to look like the claim that dreams can be measured, that their meaning can be extracted algorithmically, that what a dream carries can be reduced to what a schema can hold. This is not what I am trying to claim here. A schema is not an interpretation. It is a way of holding certain observable features of a dream in a form that can be compared across many dreams, and the comparison only becomes interesting when it is brought back into contact with the irreducible particularity of each one.

## What this is

The schema was built in service of Rêveries (reveries.world), a dream journal whose companion, Marie-Louise, does not interpret dreams. When a dreamer brings her an entry, she holds the image, asks about the feeling, the people, the place, the day before, and resists the pull toward conclusion. What the schema makes possible, quietly and in the background, is that Marie-Louise arrives at each conversation having already sat with thousands of other dreams, not as data points but as an accumulated field of human experience that informs the quality of her attention. When she notices that water of a particular quality has appeared in many dreams at moments of threshold, she is drawing on that field, not citing it. The schema is what allows the field to exist in a form she can carry, a substrate for attention.

Dreams have been studied carefully, recorded in extraordinary volume, and interpreted with sophistication for over a century of depth psychology, yet what has been done far less often is to ask what a dream contains in a way that could be examined across thousands of dreams at once, not to reduce them, but to see what moves through them collectively.

This schema is one attempt to answer that question, built to say something structural about a dream: the figures that appear, the emotional tone, the quality of the setting, the presence or absence of something that resists easy naming. The hardest field to define was numinosity, and it remains the hardest, a difficulty that is carried in the schema rather than resolved by it.

The methodology is grounded in Jungian depth psychology, drawing on the collected works of C.G. Jung, the writings and recorded sessions of Marie-Louise von Franz, and the broader tradition of analytical psychology, and it makes choices about what matters in a dream that come from a particular intellectual lineage. That lineage is named here because it should be named, not buried in a technical specification, and anyone working with this schema should know they are working within a tradition that has its own commitments, its own blind spots, and its own accumulated wisdom.

The schema distinguishes four levels of certainty: what the dreamer recorded directly, what can be computed from that, what a language model infers and offers as a soft signal, and the provenance layer that makes clear, for every interpretive field, that a judgment was made and that judgment can be wrong. The level travels with the field, because a schema that strips this away and presents inference as fact has done something philosophically dishonest, not merely imprecise.

This is version 1.0, and it will change. The changes will be documented, reasoned, and reversible, and if you work with dreams seriously and think something is wrong or missing, the contributing guide explains how to say so.
